Understanding Positive Definite Matrices

I discuss a geometric interpretation of positive definite matrices and how this relates to various properties of them, such as positive eigenvalues, positive determinants, and decomposability. I also discuss their importance in quadratic programming.

Published

27 February 2022

A real-valued matrix A\mathbf{A}A is positive definite if, for every real-valued vector x\mathbf{x}x,

x⊤Ax>0,x≠0.(1) \mathbf{x}^{\top} \mathbf{A} \mathbf{x} \gt 0, \quad \mathbf{x} \neq \mathbf{0}. \tag{1} x⊤Ax>0,x​=0.(1)

The matrix is positive semi-definite if

x⊤Ax≥0.(2) \mathbf{x}^{\top} \mathbf{A} \mathbf{x} \geq 0. \tag{2} x⊤Ax≥0.(2)

If the inequalities are reversed, then A\mathbf{A}A is negative definite or negative semi-definite respectively. If no inequality holds, the matrix is indefinite. These definitions can be generalized to complex-valued matrices, but in this post, I will assume real numbers.

I know these definitions by rote because the notion of positive definiteness comes up a lot in statistics, where covariance matrices are always positive semi-definite. However, this concept has long felt slippery. I could define and use it, but I didn’t feel like I had any real intuition for it. What do these definitions represent geometrically? What constraints imply that covariance matrices are always positive semi-definite? Why are these matrices featured heavily in quadratic programming? Why is the space of all positive semi-definite matrices a cone? The goal of this post is to fix this deficit in understanding.

This post will rely heavily on a geometrical understanding of matrices and dot products.

Multivariate positive numbers

To start, let’s put aside the definition of positive semi-definite matrices and instead focus on an intuitive idea of what they are.

Positive definite matrices can be viewed as multivariate analogs to strictly positive real numbers, while positive semi-definite matrices can be viewed as multivariate analogs to nonnegative real numbers. Consider a scalar aaa. If a<0a < 0a<0, then the sign of aba bab will depend on the sign of bbb. However, if a>0a > 0a>0, then aba bab will have the same sign as bbb. We can think about aaa and bbb as 111-vectors and about the product ababab as a dot product,

a⊤b=[a]⊤[b]=ab.(3) \mathbf{a}^{\top} \mathbf{b} = \begin{bmatrix} a \end{bmatrix}^{\top} \begin{bmatrix} b \end{bmatrix} = ab. \tag{3} a⊤b=[a​]⊤[b​]=ab.(3)

When a\mathbf{a}a is positive, then a⊤b\mathbf{a}^{\top} \mathbf{b}a⊤b will be on the same side of the origin 000 as b\mathbf{b}b. So a\mathbf{a}a does not “flip” b\mathbf{b}b about the origin. Rather, it stretches b\mathbf{b}b in the same direction (Figure 111).

Figure 1. When a>0a > 0a>0, the product ababab can be interpreted as bbb being stretched by aaa in the same direction that bbb already points, where aaa, bbb, and ababab are viewed as vectors with tails at the origin 000. On the yyy-axis, vectors are offset from 000 for visualization purposes.

Analogously, a positive definite matrix behaves like a positive number in the sense that it never flips a vector about the origin 0\mathbf{0}0. The simplest example of a positive definite matrix is a diagonal matrix that scales a vector in the direction that it already points, and the simplest example of a matrix that is not positive definite is one which simply reverses the vector (Figure 222).

Figure 2. (Left) A diagonal matrix A\mathbf{A}A with positive diagonal values does not flip x\mathbf{x}x about the origin and is thus positive definite. (Right) A diagonal matrix A\mathbf{A}A with negative values flips x\mathbf{x}x about the origin and is thus not positive definite.

However, it is less clear how to think about scenarios in which a matrix A\mathbf{A}A projects a vector in a way that does not simply scale the vector by some constant. For example, does the matrix in the left subplot of Figure 333 flip the vector x\mathbf{x}x about the origin or not? What about the matrix in the middle subplot? How can we make these intuitions precise? The answer is in the dot product. The dot product between two NNN-vectors, defined as

x⊤y=∑n=1Nxnyn,(4) \mathbf{x}^{\top} \mathbf{y} = \sum_{n=1}^N x_n y_n, \tag{4} x⊤y=n=1∑N​xn​yn​,(4)

can be viewed as vector-matrix multiplication, which in turn can be viewed as a linear projection: an NNN-vector y\mathbf{y}y is projected onto a real number line defined by the columns of a 1×N1 \times N1×N matrix x\mathbf{x}x. See my posts on matrices and the dot product if this claim does not make sense. Another definition of the dot product is that it is the product of the Euclidean magnitudes of the two vectors times the cosine of the angle between them:

x⊤y=∥x∥∥y∥cos⁡θ.(5) \mathbf{x}^{\top} \mathbf{y} = \lVert \mathbf{x} \rVert \lVert \mathbf{y} \rVert \cos \theta. \tag{5} x⊤y=∥x∥∥y∥cosθ.(5)

What these definitions imply is that two nonzero vectors x\mathbf{x}x and y\mathbf{y}y are orthogonal to each other if their dot product is zero,

x⊤y=0.(6) \mathbf{x}^{\top} \mathbf{y} = 0. \tag{6} x⊤y=0.(6)

Why? First, if their dot product is zero and if neither vector is the zero vector, then the cosine angle between the two vectors must be 90∘90^{\circ}90∘ (Figure 333, middle).

Figure 3. A vector x\mathbf{x}x is mapped by a matrix A\mathbf{A}A to a new vector Ax\mathbf{Ax}Ax. (Left) The angle between x\mathbf{x}x and Ax\mathbf{Ax}Ax is acute. (Middle) The angle between x\mathbf{x}x and Ax\mathbf{Ax}Ax is right. The angle between x\mathbf{x}x and Ax\mathbf{Ax}Ax is obtuse.

If the dot product between two nonzero vectors is positive,

x⊤y>0,(7) \mathbf{x}^{\top} \mathbf{y} > 0, \tag{7} x⊤y>0,(7)

then both vectors point in a “similar direction” in the sense that the cosine angle between them is less than 90∘90^{\circ}90∘; the angle is acute (Figure 333, left). And if the dot product is negative,

x⊤y<0,(8) \mathbf{x}^{\top} \mathbf{y} < 0, \tag{8} x⊤y<0,(8)

then both vectors point in a “dissimilar direction” in the sense that the cosine angle between them is greater than 90∘90^{\circ}90∘; the angle is obtuse (Figure 333, right).

You can probably already see where we are going with this. One geometric interpretation of positive definiteness is that A\mathbf{A}A does not “flip” x\mathbf{x}x about the origin in the sense that the cosine angle between x\mathbf{x}x and Ax\mathbf{A}\mathbf{x}Ax is between −90∘-90^{\circ}−90∘ and 90∘90^{\circ}90∘. In other words, x\mathbf{x}x and Ax\mathbf{Ax}Ax are in the same half of an NNN-dimensional hypersphere (Figure 444).

Figure 4. (Left) The cosine function is positive between −π/2-\pi/2−π/2 and π/2\pi/2π/2 or between −90∘-90^{\circ}−90∘ and 90∘90^{\circ}90∘. (Right) A positive semi-definite matrix A\mathbf{A}A maps a vector x\mathbf{x}x to a new location Ax\mathbf{Ax}Ax such that the cosine angle between x\mathbf{x}x and Ax\mathbf{Ax}Ax is between −90∘-90^{\circ}−90∘ and 90∘90^{\circ}90∘.

In my mind, Figure 444 captures the geometric interpretation of Equation 111. It’s a bit easier to see this when we make the location of the dot product operator more explicit, i.e.

x⋅Ax>0.(9) \mathbf{x} \cdot \mathbf{A}\mathbf{x} \gt 0. \tag{9} x⋅Ax>0.(9)

(Here, a⋅b\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}a⋅b is another common notation for the dot product.) This is similar to defining a real number as a number aaa such that

b(ab)>0,(10) b(ab) > 0, \tag{10} b(ab)>0,(10)

for any number b≠0b \neq 0b​=0. To summarize everything so far, an N×NN \times NN×N matrix is positive definite if it maps any vector into the same half of an NNN-dimensional hypersphere. Geometrically, this is analogous to a positive number (Figure 111).

Examples

Now that we have a definition of and some geometric intuition for positive semi-definite matrices, let’s look at some examples. First, consider the matrix

A=[1003].(11) \mathbf{A} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix}. \tag{11} A=[10​03​].(11)

This is positive semi-definite since

[x1x2][1003][x1x2]=x12+3x22≥0.(12) \begin{bmatrix} x_1 & x_2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix} = x_1^2 + 3 x_2^2 \geq 0. \tag{12} [x1​​x2​​][10​03​][x1​x2​​]=x12​+3x22​≥0.(12)

We can see that it would be straightforward to prove that any diagonal matrix with positive elements is positive semi-definite. Since the diagonal elements in a diagonal matrix are the matrix’s eigenvalues, we might wonder if positive semi-definite matrices have nonnegative eigenvalues. Indeed, they do, which we’ll see in the next section.

Next, consider a singular matrix,

A=[1111].(13) \mathbf{A} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}. \tag{13} A=[11​11​].(13)

This is positive semi-definite since

[x1x2][1111][x1x2]=(x12+x2)2≥0.(14) \begin{bmatrix} x_1 & x_2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix} = (x_1^2 + x_2)^2 \geq 0. \tag{14} [x1​​x2​​][11​11​][x1​x2​​]=(x12​+x2​)2≥0.(14)

Thus, a matrix does not need to be invertible to be positive semi-definite. However, we will see in the next section that a positive definite matrix must be invertible. As you might have guessed, this is because positive definite matrices must have strictly positive eigenvalues.

Now let’s look at an example of an indefinite matrix:

A=[1−22−1].(15) \mathbf{A} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -2 \\ 2 & -1 \end{bmatrix}. \tag{15} A=[12​−2−1​].(15)

This is indefinite since

[x1x2][1−22−1][x1x2]=x12−x22≱0.(16) \begin{bmatrix} x_1 & x_2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -2 \\ 2 & -1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix} = x_1^2 - x_2^2 \ngeq 0. \tag{16} [x1​​x2​​][12​−2−1​][x1​x2​​]=x12​−x22​≱0.(16)

However, a matrix need not have negative entries to be indefinite. Consider this matrix with only positive values:

A=[3554].(17) \mathbf{A} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 5 \\ 5 & 4 \end{bmatrix}. \tag{17} A=[35​54​].(17)

Then

[x1x2][3554][x1x2]=3x12+10x1x2+4x22.(18) \begin{bmatrix} x_1 & x_2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 5 \\ 5 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix} = 3 x_1^2 + 10 x_1 x_2 + 4 x_2^2. \tag{18} [x1​​x2​​][35​54​][x1​x2​​]=3x12​+10x1​x2​+4x22​.(18)

Is this quadratic equation always positive? It’s hard to say because it’s not clear if the positive terms will grow faster than the potentially negative term, 10x1x210 x_1 x_210x1​x2​. (In fact, this matrix is not positive semi-definite because for large enough values of x\mathbf{x}x, the quadratic x⊤Ax<0\mathbf{x}^{\top} \mathbf{A} \mathbf{x} \lt 0x⊤Ax<0.)

To me, these examples make clear that we need to understand some general mathematical properties that make identifying positive definiteness and positive semi-definiteness easier. Let’s do that now.

Properties

Positive semi-definite matrices have some convenient and important properties that make identifying and working with them relatively easy. I’ll only mention a few that come up often (for me). See (Bhatia, 2009) for a detailed discussion and rigorous proofs.

Eigenvalues. First, a positive definite matrix has strictly positive eigenvalues. Let A\mathbf{A}A be an N×NN \times NN×N matrix. Recall that the definition of the nnn-th eigenvector vn\mathbf{v}_nvn​ with eigenvalue λn\lambda_nλn​ is

Avn=λnvn.(19) \mathbf{A} \mathbf{v}_n = \lambda_n \mathbf{v}_n. \tag{19} Avn​=λn​vn​.(19)

We can take the dot product between vn\mathbf{v}_nvn​ and both sides of Equation 191919 to get

vn⊤Avn=λnvn⊤vn.(20) \mathbf{v}_n^{\top} \mathbf{A} \mathbf{v}_n = \lambda_n \mathbf{v}_n^{\top} \mathbf{v}_n. \tag{20} vn⊤​Avn​=λn​vn⊤​vn​.(20)

If A\mathbf{A}A is positive definite, then clearly λn>0\lambda_n \gt 0λn​>0. Note that λn≠0\lambda_n \neq 0λn​​=0, since this would imply that vn⊤Avn=0\mathbf{v}_n^{\top} \mathbf{A} \mathbf{v}_n = 0vn⊤​Avn​=0, which breaks our assumption about A\mathbf{A}A. In words, an eigenvector vn\mathbf{v}_nvn​ of a matrix A\mathbf{A}A is simply scaled by λn\lambda_nλn​ when transformed by A\mathbf{A}A (Figure 555). Thus, λn\lambda_nλn​ cannot be negative or else Avn\mathbf{A}\mathbf{v}_nAvn​ could represent flipping vn\mathbf{v}_nvn​ about the origin. Note that if A\mathbf{A}A is positive semi-definite rather than positive definite, then the eigenvalues are nonnegative rather than strictly positive.

Figure 5. A matrix A\mathbf{A}A with eigenvector vn\mathbf{v}_nvn​ and eigenvalue λn\lambda_nλn​. By definition, an eigenvector is a vector such that Avn\mathbf{Av}_nAvn​ points in the same direction as vn\mathbf{v}_nvn​ but with a magnitude defined by the eigenvalue λn\lambda_nλn​.

This is geometrically intuitive to me. We can think of the eigenvectors as defining how a matrix maps any vector transformed by the matrix, and the eigenvalues define the sign of each eigenvector. So if a matrix is to behave like a multivariate positive number, then the eigenvalues should be nonnegative so that the “action” of the matrix keeps the vector on the same side of the origin (as defined by Equation 111 and visualized in Figure 444).

We now have a straightforward way to verify that the matrix in Equation 171717 is not positive semi-definite. It has a negative eigenvalue:

>>> A = np.array([[3, 5], [5, 4]])
>>> eigvals, eigvecs = np.linalg.eig(A)
>>> any(eigvals < 0)
True

We can also easily check that the matrix in Equation 131313 is positive semi-definite, since it has nonnegative eigenvalues, but that it is not positive definite, since one eigenvalue is zero.

Determinant. Another way to think about this is via the determinant. Recall that the determinant of a matrix is the product of its eigenvalues,

det⁡(A)=∏n=1Nλn.(21) \det(\mathbf{A}) = \prod_{n=1}^N \lambda_n. \tag{21} det(A)=n=1∏N​λn​.(21)

The geometric intuition for the determinant of an N×MN \times MN×M matrix is that it is the signed scale factor representing how much a matrix transforms the volume an NNN-cube into an MMM-dimensional parallelepiped. So if all the eigenvalues of a positive semi-definite matrix are nonnegative, then the determinant of a positive semi-definite matrix is nonnegative. This is just another way of quantifying what we mean by whether or not a matrix “flips” a vector about the origin.

So here is a second check that the matrix in Equation 171717 is not positive semi-definite. It has a negative determinant:

>>> A = np.array([[3, 5], [5, 4]])
>>> np.linalg.det(A)  # 3*4 - 5*5 = -13
-13.0

Decomposition. Recall that every positive number aaa has two square roots, a\sqrt{a}a​ and −a-\sqrt{a}−a​. The positive square root is the principal square root, which is typically what is meant when someone refers to “the square root of aaa”. Analogously, every symmetric matrix A\mathbf{A}A is positive semi-definite if and only if it can be decomposed as a product

A=B⊤B.(22) \mathbf{A} = \mathbf{B}^{\top} \mathbf{B}. \tag{22} A=B⊤B.(22)

Thus, B\mathbf{B}B is the multivariate analog to the principal square root a\sqrt{a}a​.

Let’s see why this is true. In the “only if” direction, let A=B⊤B\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{B}^{\top} \mathbf{B}A=B⊤B. Then

x⊤Ax=x⊤B⊤Bx=∥Bx∥22≥0.(23) \begin{aligned} \mathbf{x}^{\top} \mathbf{A} \mathbf{x} &= \mathbf{x}^{\top} \mathbf{B}^{\top} \mathbf{B} \mathbf{x} \\ &= \lVert \mathbf{B} \mathbf{x} \rVert_2^2 \\ &\geq 0. \end{aligned} \tag{23} x⊤Ax​=x⊤B⊤Bx=∥Bx∥22​≥0.​(23)

This is similar to an argument that if a=aaa = \sqrt{a}\sqrt{a}a=a​a​, then aaa is positive (for real numbers).

In the “if” direction, let A\mathbf{A}A be a symmetric, positive semi-definite matrix. Since it is symmetric, it has an eigendecomposition

A=QΛQ⊤,(24) \mathbf{A} = \mathbf{Q}\boldsymbol{\Lambda}\mathbf{Q}^{\top}, \tag{24} A=QΛQ⊤,(24)

where Q\mathbf{Q}Q is a N×NN \times NN×N matrix of eigenvectors. Since A\mathbf{A}A is positive semi-definite, all the eigenvalues are nonnegative. Thus, we can use Λ1/2\boldsymbol{\Lambda}^{1/2}Λ1/2 to denote a diagonal matrix, where each element in Λ1/2\boldsymbol{\Lambda}^{1/2}Λ1/2 is the square root of the associated eigenvalue in Λ\boldsymbol{\Lambda}Λ. Then A\mathbf{A}A can be written as

A=QΛ1/2Λ1/2Q⊤,(25) \mathbf{A} = \mathbf{Q}\boldsymbol{\Lambda}^{1/2}\boldsymbol{\Lambda}^{1/2}\mathbf{Q}^{\top}, \tag{25} A=QΛ1/2Λ1/2Q⊤,(25)

and clearly B=Λ1/2Q⊤\mathbf{B} = \boldsymbol{\Lambda}^{1/2} \mathbf{Q}^{\top}B=Λ1/2Q⊤. This is why a convenient and fast way to construct a positive semi-definite matrix in code is to simply compute B⊤B\mathbf{B}^{\top} \mathbf{B}B⊤B for a full-rank matrix B\mathbf{B}B.

>>> B = np.random.randn(100, 10)  # Construct full-rank matrix B.
>>> A = B.T @ B                   # Construct positive semi-definite matrix A.
>>> L = np.linalg.cholesky(A)     # Verify A is positive semi-definite.

I’m glossing over some finer points about the rank of B\mathbf{B}B and the Cholesky decomposition here, but hopefully the main idea is clear.

We can now see why covariance matrices are positive semi-definite. The sample covariance is obviously positive semi-definite using the property above. If X\mathbf{X}X is a full-rank design matrix with NNN samples and PPP predictors, then the sample covariance matrix,

Σ^=1NX⊤X,(26) \hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}} = \frac{1}{N} \mathbf{X}^{\top} \mathbf{X}, \tag{26} Σ^=N1​X⊤X,(26)

is positive semi-definite matrix. We can also prove this is true for the population covariance. Let x\mathbf{x}x be a random variable with mean μ\boldsymbol{\mu}μ and population covariance

Σ=E[(x−μ)(x−μ)⊤].(27) \boldsymbol{\Sigma} = \mathbb{E}\left[(\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu})(\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu})^{\top}\right]. \tag{27} Σ=E[(x−μ)(x−μ)⊤].(27)

For any non-random vector, w\mathbf{w}w, we have

w⊤Σw=w⊤E[(x−μ)(x−μ)⊤]w=E[w⊤(x−μ)(x−μ)⊤w]=E[(w⊤(x−μ))2]≥0.(28) \begin{aligned} \mathbf{w}^{\top} \boldsymbol{\Sigma} \mathbf{w} &= \mathbf{w}^{\top} \mathbb{E}\left[(\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu})(\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu})^{\top}\right] \mathbf{w} \\ &= \mathbb{E}\left[\mathbf{w}^{\top} (\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu})(\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu})^{\top} \mathbf{w} \right] \\ &= \mathbb{E}\left[ \left(\mathbf{w}^{\top} (\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu})\right)^2 \right] \\ &\geq 0. \end{aligned} \tag{28} w⊤Σw​=w⊤E[(x−μ)(x−μ)⊤]w=E[w⊤(x−μ)(x−μ)⊤w]=E[(w⊤(x−μ))2]≥0.​(28)

This works because the dot product is commutative. This is a result that is intuitive once we think of positive semi-definite matrices as nonnegative numbers. Since variance σ2\sigma^2σ2 is nonnegative, it makes sense that covariance Σ\boldsymbol{\Sigma}Σ is positive semi-definite. And just as we can take the square root of σ2\sigma^2σ2, we can decompose the covariance Σ\boldsymbol{\Sigma}Σ.

Quadratic programming

There is a second geometric way to think about positive definite matrices: they are useful for optimization because an equation in quadratic form is convex when the matrix is symmetric and positive definite. Recall that a quadratic equation is any equation that can be written in the following form:

f(x)=12ax2−bx+c.(29) f(x) = \frac{1}{2} ax^2 - bx + c. \tag{29} f(x)=21​ax2−bx+c.(29)

(As we will see in a moment, the coefficients 1/21/21/2 and −1-1−1 just make the math a little cleaner for our purposes.) The generalization of a quadratic equation to multiple variables is any scalar-valued function in quadratic form:

f(x)=12x⊤Ax−b⊤x+c.(30) f(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{2} \mathbf{x}^{\top} \mathbf{A} \mathbf{x} - \mathbf{b}^{\top} \mathbf{x} + c. \tag{30} f(x)=21​x⊤Ax−b⊤x+c.(30)

What does this quadratic form have to do with definiteness? We will show that if A\mathbf{A}A is a symmetric, positive-definite matrix, then f(x)f(\mathbf{x})f(x) in Equation 303030 is minimized by the solution to Ax=b\mathbf{A} \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}Ax=b. This a very cool connection. It means that there are two ways of viewing A\mathbf{A}A. Let the notation x⋆\mathbf{x}^{\star}x⋆ denote the vector x\mathbf{x}x that solves Ax=b\mathbf{A}\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}Ax=b. The claim is that

x⋆=arg⁡ ⁣min⁡x{12x⊤Ax−b⊤x+c}.(31) \mathbf{x}^{\star} = \arg\!\min_{\mathbf{x}} \left\{ \frac{1}{2} \mathbf{x}^{\top} \mathbf{A} \mathbf{x} - \mathbf{b}^{\top} \mathbf{x} + c \right\}. \tag{31} x⋆=argxmin​{21​x⊤Ax−b⊤x+c}.(31)

This is fairly easy to prove, but let’s start with some geometric intuition.

First, observe that our claim is true in the univariate case. The first-order necessary condition for optimality is that the first derivative of fff is zero. We can find the critical points of fff by taking the derivative of fff, setting it equal to zero, and solving for xxx:

f′(x)=0  ⟹  x=ba.(32) f^{\prime}(x) = 0 \implies x = \frac{b}{a}. \tag{32} f′(x)=0⟹x=ab​.(32)

So x⋆=b/ax^{\star} = b/ax⋆=b/a satisfies the first-order condition for optimality. But is it a minimum? Let’s assume that a>0a > 0a>0, the univariate analog to positive definiteness. Then the second derivative f′′(x)=af^{\prime\prime}(x) = af′′(x)=a is always positive:

a>0  ⟹  f′′(x)>0.(33) a \gt 0 \implies f^{\prime\prime}(x) \gt 0. \tag{33} a>0⟹f′′(x)>0.(33)

Thus, x⋆x^{\star}x⋆ is a local minimum by the second derivative test. But notice we can make a stronger claim: fff is a convex function, since f′′(x)>0f^{\prime\prime}(x) \gt 0f′′(x)>0 for all xxx. So x⋆x^{\star}x⋆ is a global minimum (Figure 666).

Figure 6. The quadratic function f(x)=(1/2)ax2−bx+cf(x) = (1/2) a x^2 - bx + cf(x)=(1/2)ax2−bx+c for values a=6a = 6a=6, b=4b = 4b=4, and c=−2c = -2c=−2. The value x=b/a=2/3x = b/a = 2/3x=b/a=2/3 minimizes f(x)f(x)f(x).

Now let’s look at the multivariate case. Perhaps you have already guessed what I will write next: just as aaa must be positive for f(x)f(x)f(x) to be the global minimum at x=b/ax = b/ax=b/a, the matrix A\mathbf{A}A must be symmetric and positive definite for f(x)f(\mathbf{x})f(x) to attain the global minimum at x⋆\mathbf{x}^{\star}x⋆. Let’s see that. Let A\mathbf{A}A be a symmetric and positive matrix. Since it is symmetric, A=A⊤\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{A}^{\top}A=A⊤, and the derivative is

f′(x)=12(A+A⊤)x−b=Ax−b.(34) \begin{aligned} f^{\prime}(\mathbf{x}) &= \frac{1}{2} \left( \mathbf{A} + \mathbf{A}^{\top} \right) \mathbf{x} - \mathbf{b} \\ &= \mathbf{A} \mathbf{x} - \mathbf{b}. \end{aligned} \tag{34} f′(x)​=21​(A+A⊤)x−b=Ax−b.​(34)

See Equation 818181 in (Petersen et al., 2008) for the derivative of x⊤Ax\mathbf{x}^{\top} \mathbf{A} \mathbf{x}x⊤Ax. So we can see that x⋆\mathbf{x}^{\star}x⋆ satisfies the first-order condition for optimality. And since the Hessian is positive definite, f′′(x)=Af^{\prime\prime}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{A}f′′(x)=A,

A≻0  ⟹  f′′(x)≻0,(35) \mathbf{A} \succ 0 \implies f^{\prime\prime}(\mathbf{x}) \succ 0, \tag{35} A≻0⟹f′′(x)≻0,(35)

then x⋆\mathbf{x}^{\star}x⋆ is a local minimum by the second partial derivative test. Finally, we can repeat our stronger claim: since the Hessian of fff is a positive definite for all x\mathbf{x}x, then fff is a convex function, and x⋆\mathbf{x}^{\star}x⋆ is the global minimum.

All that said, the above doesn’t provide much intuition as to why positive definiteness is the key property. Let’s try a different approach, which I found in (Shewchuk, 1994). Suppose that A\mathbf{A}A is a symmetric matrix, and let x=x⋆\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{x}^{\star}x=x⋆. Let e\mathbf{e}e be an “error term” in the sense that it is a random perturbation to x\mathbf{x}x. Then

Positive semi-definite cone

As a final note, the class of symmetric positive semi-definite matrices forms a “cone”, sometimes called the PSD cone. Mathematically, this is straightforward easy to see. Let A\mathbf{A}A and B\mathbf{B}B be two positive semi-definite matrices (not necessarily symmetric), and let α>0\alpha > 0α>0 and β=1−α\beta = 1 - \alphaβ=1−α. Then

x⊤(αA+βB)x=αx⊤Ax+βx⊤Bx≥0.(40) \mathbf{x}^{\top} (\alpha \mathbf{A} + \beta \mathbf{B}) \mathbf{x} = \alpha \mathbf{x}^{\top} \mathbf{A} \mathbf{x} + \beta \mathbf{x}^{\top} \mathbf{B} \mathbf{x} \geq 0. \tag{40} x⊤(αA+βB)x=αx⊤Ax+βx⊤Bx≥0.(40)

So the convex combination of two positive semi-definite matrices is itself a positive semi-definite matrix. This means that we can think of the space of all positive semi-definite matrices as a convex space. We can even visualize this in three dimensions for a two-dimensional matrix. Consider any arbitrary two dimensional symmetric matrix,

Conclusion

An N×NN \times NN×N positive definite matrix is analogous to a strictly positive number but in NNN-dimensional space, while a positive semi-definite matrix is analogous to a nonnegative number. This is because positive definite and positive semi-definite matrices transform vectors such that the output vector is in the same half of an NNN-dimensional hypersphere as the input vector. Positive semi-definite matrices have important properties, such nonnegative eigenvalues and a nonnegative determinant. Finally, positive definite matrices are important in optimization because a quadratic form with an N×NN \times NN×N positive matrix is a convex function in N+1N+1N+1 dimensions
